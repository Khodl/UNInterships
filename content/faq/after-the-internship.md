A UN internship is often the first step toward a career in international organizations. Here's what to expect once it ends.

## Can you get hired after an internship?

Yes, but not directly. Most UN agencies have a **mandatory break-in-service** policy — you must wait a period (usually 1 to 6 months) before being eligible for a paid position within the same agency.

This means you cannot be immediately converted from intern to staff. However, many former interns are later recruited through competitive processes.

## Common next steps

### Consultancy contracts
Short-term contracts (3-12 months) are the most common entry point after an internship. They are project-based and often don't include benefits, but they build your UN track record.

### UN Volunteers (UNV)
The UNV program offers volunteer assignments with a living allowance. It's an excellent way to gain field experience and is open to candidates with limited professional experience.

### Young Professionals Programme (YPP)
The UN Secretariat's YPP is a competitive exam for candidates under 32 from underrepresented countries. Passing it leads to a fixed-term P-1 or P-2 position.

### Junior Professional Officer (JPO)
JPO programs are sponsored by member states. If your country participates, you can apply for 2-3 year assignments at the P-2 level in various UN agencies.

## Building your profile

To strengthen your candidacy for future UN positions:

- **Ask for a reference letter** from your supervisor before your internship ends
- **Stay connected** — join UN alumni networks and LinkedIn groups
- **Gain field experience** — agencies value work in developing countries
- **Learn another UN language** — this significantly expands your options
- **Keep applying** — persistence is key in the UN recruitment process
